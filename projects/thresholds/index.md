# Thresholds
**Year:** 2006
**Type:** Speculative Installation
**Location:** LSU College of Art and Design Atrium
**Collaborators:** Wes Michaels
**Phase:** Phase I — The Digital Threshold (2005–2012)

## Description

A high-contrast mural painted on the atrium wall served as a visual datum. A camera performed continuous blob detection, identifying regions of contrast difference, and custom Processing software converted these contrast gradients into isolines displayed in real time on an adjacent monitor. As light shifted and pedestrians moved through the space, they disrupted the contrast field, generating ephemeral isoline landscapes. Occupants became unwitting topographic events.

## Contribution

Representation can be dynamic rather than static, responding to conditions rather than fixing them. Sensing and visualization can form a continuous circuit, collapsing the temporal gap between data collection and communication. The contour line, landscape architecture's most fundamental notation, is revealed as an active interpretation of gradient and threshold, not neutral transcription of terrain. What the instrument sees depends on the datum against which it reads difference.
